Eat a raisin mindfully. Pick it up, noticing how it feels in your hand. Look at it carefully; see the asymmetries and think about the changes the grape went through. Roll the raisin in your fingers and notice the texture; try to describe it. Bring the raisin up to your mouth, paying attention to how it feels to move your hand that way. Smell the raisin; what does it remind you of? How does a raisin smell? Notice that you're beginning to salivate, and see how that feels. Open your mouth and put the raisin in, taking time to think about how the raisin feels to your tongue. Chew slowly, noticing how the texture and even the taste of the raisin change as you chew it. Are there little seeds or stems? How is the inside different from the outside? Finally, swallow.

A Cause vs a Trigger
People self-harm for many different reasons. It doesn’t always involve cutting. Often, self-harm is a way to deal with difficult emotions. Finding out why you self-injure (e.g. the cause) and learning other coping strategies can take time. This is why identifying ‘triggers’ of self-destructive behaviour can help. Once you know what these are, you can avoid them or put a plan into place so that if the trigger happens you will be safer.
Possible triggers can be:
A specific person
Alcohol and/or drugs
Being alone in the evening
Lack of sleep
Reading about other people’s self-harm behaviour
Work or school stress
Arguments
Social media/comparing yourself to others
Self-harm management strategies
  • Write yourself a list of reasons to not self-harm, examples could be:
    “Cutting affects other people too, and can be bad for my relationships”
    “I want to be able to go swimming again.”
    “I haven’t harmed for 12 days. I want to make it to a month”
    “I just keep going once I begin”
    “I’ll regret the cuts once I’ve made them”
    “It doesn’t help in the long run. I can harm now but I’ll need to do it again in a couple of days.”
    “I (would) hate being in hospital”
    “Distracting myself for 15 minutes, e.g. riding the wave, sometimes means the urge passes”
    “Some consequences I wouldn’t want, like permanent nerve damage”
  • Learn CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy) or DBT (Dialectical Behaviour Therapy) techniques. http://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/...
  • When you’re feeling good write yourself a letter you can read when you want to self-harm
  • Write down why you do not deserve to be hurt
  • Ask friends and family to write down nice things about you on sticky notes and stick them somewhere safe. Try not to look at them unless you need to self-harm, so that they are a surprise!
  • Avoid shops that sell things you might use to harm yourself
  • Ask GP to give you weekly prescriptions or pick up medication for two days at a time from the chemist
  • Do not stockpile medication (prescribed or over the counter)
  • Reduce your commitments in the next few days and take some self-care time
  • Keep a chart – add a star for each day / hour you have not self-harmed. If you do self-harm, just leave a space and start again
  • Keep things you harm yourself with in a locked cupboard or in a box with tape around it. It gives you time to think between wanting to self-harm and doing it.
  • Use any of the crisis aversion ideas for avoiding self-harm to try and delay it for a while till the urge passes (people call it riding the wave)
  • Record yourself saying all of the reasons you shouldn’t self-harm on your phone

After you've harmed
  1. Behaviour Chain Analysis – identify what led you to self-harm.
    http://www.dbtselfhelp.com/...
    A quick summary: describe the event of self-harm; describe what lead up to it, this may be people or your environment; describe any vulnerability factors that may have exacerbated the precipitating events, e.g. alcohol, loneliness; write out the immediate consequences of the behaviour, and any later ones; circle any parts of your chain which, if you had done something differently, may not have led you to self-harm; plan out a prevention strategy, identifying triggers to stay away from; Finally, decide if you need to do anything to reduce any of the negative consequences e.g. talking/apologising to a family member or friend, who may have had to clean up blood/call an ambulance etc.
  2. Practice radical acceptance
    http://www.dbtselfhelp.com/...
    Basically, accept what has happened, don’t let it lower your self-esteem and remember that you alone have the power to stop making yourself feel guilty for what happened. If you feel less guilt, it’s easier to see why you deserve not to be harmed, and avoid self-harm in the future.
  3. The acronym, PLEASE Master could help
    Physical iLlness - Be aware of and try to alleviate
    Eat healthy (and drink enough water)
    Avoid mood altering drugs
    Sleep enough (suggestions here)
    Exercise
    Mastery, do something every day that gives you a sense of competence or achievement.
  4. FAST is the acronym for self-respect
    be Fair to yourself
    no Apologies for things that you couldn't help Stick to your values be Truthful

Try this daily mood booster
Every day, find three people and wish to yourself that they have a nice day. They can be strangers on the street, acquaintances, family of friends. It often seems weird or fake to say nice things (especially to strangers) but give just thinking them a try; you may end up feeling more positive yourself!

Are you getting enough light?
Although it is unlikely that you have SAD (Seasonal Affective Disorder) the lack of natural light in winter could be lowering your mood. Getting a 'Lumie' alarm clock or SAD light could help make it through the winter months. (These lights have more lux – a measure or light intensity - than normal lights, so mimic the effects of sunlight)
